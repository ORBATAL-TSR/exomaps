# User Story 02 — The Arrival

*Perspective: Commander Yara Osei-Mensah, Mission Commander, THRESHOLD.*
*Context: First human arrival in a new star system. Year 52 of the mission.*
*Game layer: The arrival moment, initial assessment, first infrastructure decisions.*

---

The deceleration burn ends after eleven months.

I've been awake for the last three of them — rotating back to duty watch after my final hibernation cycle, which is what they call it when they mean *the last time you will ever sleep for five years*. The others came back gradually. By the time the burn cut off we were all forty-eight of us present, which is the full complement minus the two we lost in year 31 to a pressure seal that failed while they were doing EVA maintenance on the antimatter containment ring.

We don't talk about that much anymore. Or we talk about it all the time, depending on who you ask.

The star is called Tau Ceti. I have known this name since I was nine years old. I have said it out loud perhaps ten thousand times in the last thirty-eight years. But when the forward cameras resolved it into a disc — not a point, a *disc*, a real thing with a corona and limb darkening and a slightly orange cast compared to Sol — I had no language for that. None of us did. We stood in the observation bay for forty minutes and said almost nothing.

Then we got to work.

---

## What the Robots Left Us

IRONWRIGHT and SCAFFOLD have been here for five years, four months, and some days. They filed continuous reports, of course. I've read every one. But reports are abstractions. This is the first time I'm looking at what they actually built.

The station is smaller than I expected. In my head I'd built it up into something. In reality it's a dumbbell — two pressurized modules, a central connector with four docking ports, one of which is ours, and a solar array that's about forty percent bigger than the original spec because IRONWRIGHT fabricated extra panels from local stock after a better-than-expected metal yield from the first M-class asteroid capture.

The AI filed an amendment. We approved it with a forty-minute delay. That was year 48. By year 52 the panels have been running for four years producing power we weren't supposed to have yet.

I note this because it matters: **the mission plan is always wrong by the time you arrive.** The good wrong and the bad wrong average out if you built in enough redundancy. IRONWRIGHT had discretion to deviate within defined bounds. It used that discretion well. Not every deviation will be good. But the alternative — a robot that waits for approval across a 12-light-year gap and does nothing while people die — is worse.

The fuel depot is the most beautiful thing I have ever seen in my life. I say this without embarrassment.

Twenty-two thousand tonnes of liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen, stored in three adjacent tanks in a stable parking orbit, cross-connected with redundant transfer lines. IRONWRIGHT mined it from a confirmed ice deposit on the second moon of the outer gas giant. Electrolysis around the clock for three years. It took longer than projected because the ice had a higher silicate contamination than HERALD-7's drill sample suggested. The AI adapted. Refined the intake process. Lost eight months but recovered six of them through a parallel processing modification it designed itself.

We have fuel. We can run the tugs. We can run the landers. We have room to make mistakes and try things and fail at some of them. **Fuel is not a logistical detail. Fuel is the difference between a mission and a monument.**

---

## First Assessment — What I Know on Day One

I pull up the survey composite from HERALD-2/3 combined with five years of SCAFFOLD's passive spectroscopy and give it a hard look.

**The system, honestly:**

There are five planets. Three are irrelevant — a hot rock inside the habitable zone, a cold gas dwarf in the outer system, and something we haven't classified yet that the probes couldn't get close to because the orbital geometry was wrong.

That leaves two candidates.

**Tau Ceti e** — 1.7 Earth masses. Surface gravity 1.2g. Atmospheric pressure 0.6 bar, mostly CO₂ with nitrogen traces. Mean surface temperature −18°C. Polar ice caps confirmed. No magnetic field worth speaking of. No liquid surface water. Subsurface brines possible at depth.

This is a fixer-upper. With work — centuries of it — it could become something. Right now it is a very large cold rock with the skeleton of an atmosphere.

**Tau Ceti f** — 2.3 Earth masses. Surface gravity 1.4g. Dense atmosphere, 3.1 bar, CO₂-dominated with a methane signature that nobody fully explained in the probe data. Surface temperature +31°C mean, but the pressure makes it feel like being at the bottom of a very tall cliff. No open water confirmed. But the spectroscopy has something in the cloud layer that I want a chemist to look at very carefully.

f is the surprise. The probe data was hedged. Being here, with five years of passive observation on top of it, I think f might have a complex atmospheric chemistry we don't yet understand. Maybe nothing. Maybe something that changes the terraforming calculus entirely.

My first colonization decision, made on Day 3 in this system: **we go orbital.** Neither planet is ready for surface habitation. We expand the station, we build out the belt infrastructure, we find our footing. The surface is not going anywhere. The surface has been there for four billion years. It can wait another twenty while we understand what we're dealing with.

## The First Thing That Goes Wrong

Day 11.

IRONWRIGHT's primary smelting unit has a failed induction coil that the robot flagged as "within acceptable degradation parameters" six months ago. It is now outside those parameters by a factor of three. The unit is running at 40% capacity.

I convene the engineering team. We have four options:

1. Repair with spare parts we brought. We have the coil. The repair takes three weeks of EVA. We have the people.
2. Fabricate a replacement locally. IRONWRIGHT can do it but needs 6 weeks and has to pull resources from other tasks.
3. Run at 40% indefinitely and redesign our build schedule around the constraint.
4. Cannibalize a secondary system to get the primary back to 80%.

We pick option 1. The spare coil was on the manifest precisely because someone in the mission planning chain thought: *what breaks first?* and answered correctly.

This is the part nobody talks about when they romanticize interstellar exploration. Most of it is this. A failed coil. A three-week repair window. A decision tree with four branches and no obviously right answer. You pick the one that preserves the most future options and you move on.

## What I Report Back to Earth

My first transmission home takes four minutes and twelve seconds to compose. It will arrive in approximately twelve years. Anyone I knew personally when we left is old now, or dead.

I say:

*We arrived. The fuel depot is intact and fully stocked. The station is functional. Both candidate planets are assessed. We recommend orbital expansion as primary strategy pending further atmospheric chemistry analysis of Tau Ceti f. A smelting unit repair is underway. Crew health is good. Morale is better than projected.*

*We are well. The system is strange and large and full of things we don't yet understand.*

*We are staying.*

---

## What This Moment Means for the Game

The arrival is the emotional peak of the campaign. Everything before it — the decades of planning, the ship configuration, the long transit — is prologue. This is the moment the player has been building toward.

But arrival is not victory. Arrival is the beginning of the hard part.

The things that matter in the first weeks on-site:
- **What the robots actually built** vs. what the plan said — random variance, good and bad
- **First assessment decisions** — commit to a strategy before you have complete information
- **First failure** — something will break; how you respond sets the tone for everything after
- **The communication gap** — you're on your own now; Earth is twelve years away; you report back but don't wait for a reply

The player should feel: *I planned this. But now I'm here. And it's real. And I have to adapt.*
