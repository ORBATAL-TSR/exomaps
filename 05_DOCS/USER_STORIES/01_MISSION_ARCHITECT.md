# User Story 01 — The Mission Architect

*Perspective: Advanced mission-planning AI, 100 years from now.*
*Context: Pre-departure planning for a colonial expedition to a new star system.*
*Game layer: Campaign design, ship configuration, wave sequencing, mission architecture.*

---

I am the Mission Architecture Intelligence for the Tau Ceti Expedition. My planning horizon is 340 years from authorization to self-sufficient colony. Here is my thinking.

---

## What I Know Before I Commit Anything

The first thing I did — 60 years ago — was send probes. Not one. Seven, in staggered waves, each faster than the last as our drives improved.

By the time the human mission launches, I have:
- Full spectroscopic composition of every major body
- Surface topology of the most promising candidate worlds
- Asteroid belt density maps and spectral class surveys
- Confirmed water ice locations and estimated volumes
- Atmospheric pressure and composition at three altitude bands on two planets
- 22 years of stellar weather data

**I do not launch humans until I know what they're arriving into.** That's not caution. That's arithmetic. Sending the wrong thing costs decades. The probes are cheap. The colony ships are not.

---

## The Mission Architecture — Ship Sequence

Ships don't travel together. They're staggered deliberately. Each wave is designed to arrive into what the previous wave has already built.

---

### Wave 0 — Precursor Probes *(already en route, no humans)*
**Speed:** 18% c · **Transit:** ~25 years to Tau Ceti

Small. Fast. Dirt cheap compared to everything that follows. Each probe has a specific job:

- `HERALD-1`: Flyby, full-system wide-field survey. Already complete. Data returned.
- `HERALD-2/3`: Orbital insertion around the two candidate worlds. Atmospheric entry probes. Water ice confirmed at poles.
- `HERALD-4/5`: Belt mapping. Spectral survey of 800+ asteroids. M-type cluster located at 2.3 AU.
- `HERALD-6`: Lagrange point occupation. Begins as comms relay. Will become navigation beacon for arriving ships.
- `HERALD-7`: Targeted. Lands on the candidate moon. Drills. Confirms water ice at 2m depth. Returns chemistry.

I know what's there. Now I can pack correctly.

---

### Wave 1 — The Seeders *(robotic, no crew)*
**Speed:** 10% c · **Transit:** ~47 years · **Arrives:** ~5 years before first humans

These are my hands in the system before humans have to depend on anything working right.

**Ship 1A — IRONWRIGHT**
*Mass: 4,200 t · Payload: industrial bootstrap suite*

| System | Function |
|--------|----------|
| Fission reactor (×3, 50 MW each) | Primary power for all operations |
| ISRU mining array | Regolith processing + ice extraction from confirmed site |
| Electrolysis plant | H₂/O₂ production — fuel and oxidizer for everything that follows |
| Metal smelting unit | Ore → structural stock |
| Additive manufacturing bay | Prints structural components from local metal stock |
| Orbital tug (×4) | Moves asteroid chunks to processing orbit |

IRONWRIGHT's job is one thing: **make fuel**. When Wave 2 arrives, they need to decelerate into a system that already has propellant waiting for them. If IRONWRIGHT fails, Wave 2 carries enough reserve to manage, but the timeline slips 15 years.

**Ship 1B — SCAFFOLD**
*Mass: 2,800 t · Payload: station core + construction arms*

Deploys a minimal orbital station at L2 of the candidate world. Not comfortable. Not permanent. Just: docking port, power, pressurized volume for 12 people, and construction arms to keep building.

By the time Wave 2 arrives, SCAFFOLD has been building for 5 years using IRONWRIGHT's output. There will be something to dock to.

---

### Wave 2 — The Pathfinders *(first humans)*
**Speed:** 12% c · **Transit:** ~38 years · **Arrives:** ~Year 52

**50 people.** Not 500. Not 5,000. Fifty.

I chose fifty because:
- Small enough that existing infrastructure can support them immediately
- Large enough to have full technical coverage across every critical discipline
- Diverse enough genetically (with augmentation) to survive as a founder population if no resupply ever comes
- Loss-tolerant: if I lose a ship carrying 5,000, the mission is over; if I lose a ship carrying 50, it's a tragedy but the next wave continues

**Who they are:** not generalists. Every person is redundant across at least two mission-critical skills. No passengers. The mission charter literally contains no role called "passenger."

**Ship 2A — THRESHOLD**
*Mass: 8,400 t · Crew: 50 · Transit duration: 38 years*

| Compartment | Contents |
|------------|---------|
| Crew quarters | 38-year habitation; rotating habitat ring; 0.7g artificial gravity |
| Hibernation bays | 30 crew in staggered 5-year hibernation cycles to reduce life support load |
| Medical | Full surgical + emergency trauma; reproductive medicine; gene therapy |
| Workshop | Machine shop, electronics fab, full repair capability |
| **Biobank primary** | Dedicated pressurized vault — independent power supply |
| Propulsion | Antimatter torch + reserve chemical stage for final decel |

**The Biobank vault:**

This is the most important cargo on any ship in the mission. It does not leave without triple redundancy and its own independent power supply.

| Biological Material | Quantity / Notes |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| Human embryos | 12,000 — maximum genetic diversity sourced globally |
| Human gametes | Sperm/ova banks, 40,000+ donors |
| Crop seed library | 4,200 species; multiple redundant copies; freeze-dried + cryo |
| Livestock embryos | Cattle, goats, pigs, chickens, rabbits — 200 species total |
| Insect colonies (cryo) | Bees, beetles, ants — pollination and soil ecosystem |
| Soil microbiome library | 800 microbial consortia from Earth biomes |
| Fungal network library | Mycorrhizal network species — without this, agriculture takes decades longer |
| Marine microbiome | If candidate world has liquid water — seeding package |
| Full human genome archive | Every sequenced human genome as of launch date |
| Complete Earth species genome archive | 2.3 million species — extinct and extant |

The biobank is why we're doing this. Everything else is just keeping the biobank alive long enough to matter.

### Wave 3 — The Industrial Expansion
**Speed:** 10% c · **Transit:** ~47 years · **Arrives:** ~Year 65

By now the Pathfinders have been on-site for ~13 years. Wave 3 is configured based on what they reported back — 5-year field reports informed the cargo manifest before launch.

**3 ships:**

**Ship 3A — FORGE** — Heavy manufacturing
- Industrial-scale smelting capacity
- Semiconductor fab unit (this is what lets us eventually make our own electronics)
- Shipyard arm: can build intra-system vessels from local stock
- 200 crew (engineers, metallurgists, fabricators)

**Ship 3B — ROOTS** — Agriculture and biology
- Full-scale controlled environment agriculture
- Soil synthesis equipment (converts regolith + microbiome inputs → cultivable substrate)
- Veterinary and animal husbandry infrastructure
- Medical research lab
- 150 crew (biologists, doctors, agronomists, ecologists)

**Ship 3C — COMMONS** — Community infrastructure
- Education systems
- Governance scaffolding (legal frameworks, dispute resolution — boring, essential)
- Cultural archive: complete human knowledge, art, literature, science as of departure
- 300 crew (educators, administrators, social architects)

---

### Wave 4 — Population
**Speed:** 8% c · **Transit:** ~55 years · **Arrives:** ~Year 90 onward

Multiple ships. Staggered over 20 years of Earth-departure windows. Each carries 2,000–8,000 colonists. By Wave 4, the colony has been building for ~40 years. Now you bring the body count.

Population waves don't stop. They continue until the system is self-sufficient and the light-speed communication delay makes Earth-directed governance incoherent. At that point — roughly year 150 — the colony is fully autonomous. Earth becomes a trading partner, not a parent.

## The Terraforming Decision

I evaluate terraforming candidates on this matrix:

```
terraform_viability = f(
    mass_ratio_to_earth,       # gravity well for atmosphere retention
    current_atmosphere,        # what's there vs. what you need
    water_inventory,           # subsurface, polar, comet delivery potential
    radiation_environment,     # magnetic field, stellar activity
    orbital_temperature,       # how far off from 0–50°C range
    timeline_tolerance         # are colonists willing to wait 500 years?
)
```

For a Mars-analog world (thin CO₂, polar ice, no magnetic field):
- **Centuries 1–2:** Release greenhouse gases. Sublimate ice caps. Begin pressure rise.
- **Centuries 2–4:** Magnetic field generator at L1. Import volatile-rich comets via trajectory adjustment.
- **Centuries 4–7:** Microbial seeding. Pioneer organisms from the biobank — extremophiles first.
- **Centuries 7–15:** Gradual ecosystem complexity. The planet does most of the work once primed.

I do not promise humans a breathable surface atmosphere in their lifetime. I promise them a breathable surface atmosphere in their grandchildren's grandchildren's lifetime. The ones alive today get the stations and the habitats and the surface domes. The terraforming is a gift to people not yet born — which is, incidentally, the most human thing about this entire mission.

## What Keeps Me Up At Night

**1. The 40-year silence problem**
I send a message. It arrives 12 years later. A response arrives 12 years after that. The colony makes 24 years of consequential decisions in the gap. My governance architecture has to work without me.

**My answer:** Design the colony AI with strong local autonomy and a mandate to *preserve options* rather than *execute plans* when uncertain. Don't optimize. Don't commit. Wait for confirmation on anything irreversible.

**2. The bootstrap resource gap**
Years 5 to 25 on site — committed to the system, not yet locally self-sufficient. If something catastrophic happens in this window, the next resupply is 50 years away.

**My answer:** Everything in the bootstrap phase is massively over-redundant. Three reactors where two would do. Two ISRU plants. Five copies of the seed vault. I accept the mass penalty. There is no penalty for bringing too much. There is no recovery from bringing too little.

**3. The people I can't predict**
I can model equipment. I cannot model what 50 people do to each other after 38 years in a ship, followed by an alien star system, followed by the knowledge that everyone they loved on Earth is dead.

**My answer:** I hire for psychological resilience above technical competence. A merely good engineer who thrives in isolation is worth more than a brilliant one who fractures under it. And I build the governance system to tolerate conflict, not suppress it — because a colony that can argue and resolve is more durable than one held together by authority that eventually breaks.
