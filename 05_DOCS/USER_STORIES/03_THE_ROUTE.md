# User Story 03 — The Route

*Perspective: Cargo Master Deshi Alvarado, IFB MERIDIAN SLOW, intra-system freight barge.*
*Context: Year 84 of the colony. 32 years after the Pathfinders arrived. The logistics layer is mature.*
*Game layer: Intra-system trade network, route operations, cascade failure, faction pressure.*

---

MERIDIAN SLOW has been running the M-belt to Station Kovač route for eleven years.

I say "running" loosely. The barge mostly runs itself. Ion drive, preset trajectory, automatic docking protocols. My job is supervision and decision-making when something falls outside the nominal parameters. Eleven years in, the nominal parameters cover about ninety-four percent of everything that happens. The other six percent is why you need a human on board.

The ship is four hundred and sixty metres long and carries up to eighty thousand tonnes of processed ore, refined metals, and increasingly, fabricated components. She moves at about two kilometres per second at peak, which feels heroic compared to nothing and embarrassing compared to everything else in the system. The fastest couriers pass me like I'm drifting. I wave. I don't think they see me.

The run takes about sixty days one way depending on orbital geometry. Right now we're at day 47 outbound, fully loaded, carrying:

- 24,000 t of smelted iron-nickel alloy (structural grade) from the Marta-7 platform
- 11,000 t of titanium billets from Piotr's claim — the only M-class with titanium-significant deposits in the inner belt
- 6,000 t of refined silicates bound for the ceramics kiln at Kovač's third ring
- 8,000 t of refined electrolytic oxygen, destined for the life support buffer at Kovač-Main
- And 340 tonnes of something I'm not asking questions about that a Provisional Authority inspector put on board at Marta-7 with a priority-one authorization and told me to deliver intact

The last item has been in bay seven, locked, for 47 days. I have not asked.

---

## What the Network Looks Like Now

I've been in this system since year 71. I was on Wave 3 — FORGE. I was 28 years old when we left Earth, 66 when we arrived, and I spent most of the transit asleep in five-year increments dreaming of nothing at all.

The system when I got here: one main station, four outposts, six active asteroid mines, and approximately the social dynamics of a very stressed small town.

The system now, year 84: eleven stations, forty-three active mining or processing nodes, a shipyard at Kovač that can build vessels up to my class, a fuel depot network with six tanker routes running continuously, and a population of around fourteen thousand people spread across orbits from 0.3 AU to the outer belt at 4.1 AU.

It's not tidy. It grew the way anything grows when you're solving immediate problems with whatever you have. The Marta cluster got built first because that's where the metals were. The fuel network grew to serve the Marta cluster. The ceramics operation grew because someone figured out the silicate composition of the inner belt was exceptionally pure. None of this was in the original plan. The plan was a guess at what we'd need. Reality required something different. We adapted.

There are now four distinct operational factions in this system and I do business with three of them.

**The Authority** — The original mission command structure. Still nominally in charge. Runs Kovač-Main and the three oldest stations. Has the most infrastructure, the most people, and the most accumulated political debt from thirty years of difficult decisions.

**The Marta Collective** — Started as a labor organizing dispute in year 74. The mining crews on the Marta platforms decided the resource allocation formula was unfair. They weren't entirely wrong. After two years of negotiations that went nowhere, they declared operational independence. They still sell us ore — we need their ore — but they set their own prices and they don't take policy direction from Kovač anymore. I work for the Authority but I pick up at Marta. Nobody finds this strange anymore.

**Piotr's Claim** — Officially: the Pietrowicz Independent Mining Cooperative. Actually: Benedikt Pietrowicz, his three adult children, eight extended family members, and the crews they've hired over fifteen years. Benedikt showed up in year 69 on the second population wave, looked at the belt survey data, found the titanium deposit that everyone else had flagged as "marginal," and spent four years developing it by himself before anyone else was paying attention. He is now, quietly, one of the most economically important people in the system. He answers to no one. He is extremely good at his job and extremely difficult to have a conversation with.

**The Outer Tendency** — This one worries me slightly. A loose association of colonists who settled the far outer system — a water-ice rich region at 4.8 AU that nobody else wanted — and have been developing it quietly for about six years. They are politically distinct from the Authority in ways that feel less like a labor dispute and more like a foundational disagreement about what this colony is *for*. They talk about not repeating Earth's mistakes, which is the kind of thing that sounds appealing when stated generally and gets complicated when stated specifically.

I carry their freight sometimes. They pay well. They're quiet. I don't know what's in their bays either.

---

## Day 47 — The Problem

The ship's AI wakes me at 0340 station time with a priority flag.

I pull up the board. MERIDIAN SLOW has an anomaly on the starboard fuel manifold — a micro-fracture in one of the transfer lines that feeds the main ion drive array. It's been propagating slowly for about nine days. The AI has been monitoring it and running probability models. At day 47 it crossed the threshold where continued operation has a meaningful probability of cascading into a primary drive loss.

My options:

**Option A: Continue at reduced thrust.** Route the fuel around the damaged line through the secondary manifold. Lose 30% drive efficiency. Transit time extends from 13 days remaining to about 19. Kovač will notice the delay and I'll need to justify it. The life support oxygen I'm carrying has a 6-day buffer in Kovač's system. A 6-day delay eats most of that buffer.

**Option B: Stop and repair.** We're 11 days from the nearest fuel depot at any meaningful thrust level. I have the parts — possibly. The manifest shows a replacement manifold segment in emergency stores. It was there when we left Marta-7. I need to physically verify it's still there and hasn't been cannibalized in a previous repair that I'm not thinking of right now.

**Option C: Divert to Piotr's Claim.** It's 4 days away at current trajectory with a mild correction burn. Pietrowicz has a machine shop. I've used it before. He will charge me aggressively for the service and for the fuel and I will not enjoy the negotiation but the repair will get done and I'll be back on route within 8 days total.

**Option C** is what I choose. The oxygen buffer at Kovač is not a crisis yet but it's a constraint I don't want to tighten further. The 8-day total delay keeps me inside the buffer margin. Pietrowicz will invoice the Authority and the Authority will be annoyed and I'll write a report explaining the decision chain and that will be the end of it.

I file the trajectory change with MERIDIAN SLOW's nav system, copy Kovač dispatch, copy Marta-7 for awareness, and send a short message to Pietrowicz.

He responds in forty minutes with: *Bay 3 is open. Fuel at posted rates. I know what you're going to say about the rates and the answer is no.*

I send back: *Understood.*

---

## What Happens at Piotr's

Benedikt Pietrowicz is sixty-three years old and has the hands of someone who has done physical work in low gravity for fifteen years. We have a drink in his operations office while his people work on the manifold. He drinks something he makes himself from an agricultural waste product that I've decided not to investigate too closely.

He asks about the locked bay.

I tell him I don't know what's in it.

He says he heard the Authority was negotiating a resource access agreement with the Outer Tendency and that the timing of my run — the mystery cargo, the route to Kovač — might be related.

I tell him I genuinely don't know.

He says: *You know what I've learned in fifteen years out here? The thing that matters isn't who controls what. It's who controls the routes. You control the routes, you control everything downstream. I don't have soldiers. I don't have a political party. But everyone in this system needs what I pull out of that rock, and everyone needs a machine shop they can trust, and that means everyone needs to stay on good terms with me. That's not power. That's geometry.*

I think about this for the remaining six days of the repair and the transit to Kovač.

He's not wrong.

---

## Kovač — Arrival

The oxygen delivery goes to Kovač life support and I watch the buffer tick back up to 98% on the station status board. Nobody at Kovač knows how close it got or how many contingency calculations I ran in the last eleven days. The manifest shows "minor route deviation, mechanical." The oxygen arrived. The metals arrived. The ceramics silicates arrived. Bay seven is handed off to two Authority officials who sign for it without looking at me and walk away with a cargo lifter.

I file my route report. I invoice for the Pietrowicz repair costs. I eat something in the docking bay cafeteria that is technically food and is made partly from the fungal culture substrate that ROOTS brought in Wave 3 and has been feeding this colony for twenty years.

In four days I depart for the belt again, empty, to load at Marta-7.

The same route. Sixty days. Forty-three active nodes in this system depending on a network of barges and landers and spaceplanes and tankers, most of which nobody thinks about until one of them stops.

---

## What This Moment Means for the Game

This is the middle game. The arrival drama is decades past. The player has built a network. Now they manage it.

The things that matter at this layer:
- **Route reliability** — a barge that's six days late ripples into a life support buffer crisis
- **Faction relationships** — Pietrowicz isn't your enemy or your ally; he's a node you need; keep him viable
- **The mystery cargo** — the player sets policy; the AI governor executes it; sometimes you don't know everything your own faction is doing
- **Geometry** — whoever controls the routes controls the system; this isn't metaphor, it's the claim score mechanic
- **Adaptation** — the plan from year 1 is unrecognizable by year 84; that's not failure, that's a living system

The player should feel: *I built this. It's messy and complicated and half of it wasn't in the original plan. And it's mine. And right now something is three days late and I need to figure out why.*
